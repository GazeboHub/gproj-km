Summary

This article shall specify a technique for application of the ISO topic map data model (TMDM) in a practice of creating detailed book reviews, i.e. book reports, such as may be writtten about — broadly — books addressing topics of material and technical significance. A demonstration of this technique shall be provided, in creating a book report about a book by operations researcher Jagjit Sing, historically towards a domain of practice referred to as operations research, more broadly towards design and management of, and supporting discourses about, projects in engineering.

This article is a feature of the Gazebo Project's Gazebo-KM work area, is developed in that work area's focus as specifically towards concepts of literacy.

Book Reports as Knowledge Resources

This heading entails a description of the component concepts, knowledge resources, and book reports (the latter, as detailed book reviews)

Use Case: A History of Operations Research

Citing: [Singh1972] Sing, Jagjit. Great Ideas of Operations Research. 2nd ed. 1972: Dover. ISBN 0–486–21886–4

The goal of this article, initially, was to describe a practical effort in beginning to define a topic map about operations research, such as would entail a utilization of [Singh1972] as it making an invaluable, initial keynote in the development of such a topic map. Latterly, this article becomes a sort of worksheet for developing an outline with regards to portal development in the Gazebo-KM work area.

The fortuitous matter of the author of this article having once located [Singh1972] in a used bookstore in a city in the San Joaquin Valley, in California — if it would not be denoted as if it was an ad hominem comment — perhaps it may be denoted as a matter of the fortuitous, in regards to a theory of complex causality — albeit, perhaps towards a broader, secular philosophical domain, then.

Towards the content of this article, presently, rather than as towards describing an annotations model being developed to this article:

In [Singh1972], Singh focuses the discourse about a presentation of five primary concepts, as ascribed to the domain of operations research (Singh1972, p. 20)

  • Network Analysis

  • Linear Programming

  • Games Theory and Rational Decisions

  • Queuing Theory

  • Monte Carlo Simulation

Clearly, each of those topics in itself may bear to a wide study of material in a broad domain of discourse, with regards to theoretical foundations and practical applications in each domain, as denoted. It is not as if to lessen the merits of study that this article proposes to address that the set of those concepts, as from a perspective of those concepts' rhetorical forms, may each represent, simultaneously, a concept (cf. SKOS) and a topic (cf. TMDM), as towards development of succinct knowledge resources, within digital media.

Topic Map to Mindmap Transform

cf. XSLT, as specifically with regards to data models expressed in XML syntax

The practical applicability of this heading entails a usage of existing tools, on desktop (e.g XMind) and mobile (e.g iThoughts) platforms, towards integration with presentation and editor portlets for topic maps and/or (transformable) SKOS graphs.

Concepts:

  • A mindmap node transorms to a TMDM topic, symmetically

  • A mindmap node link transforms to a TMDM association – in one of two modes of representing a mindmap node link with a TMDM association (with association types for node/subnode relations and for non-hierarchical, “cross-node links”, in the set of association types that must be defined to the output TMDM model)

  • Any TMDM association may be transformed to a node link in a Mindmap.

    • It may be recommended that in any transformstion of a topic map into a mindmap, a subset of the topic map's set of association types may be selected as a parameter to the transformation — as in using a set of URI, each identifying an association type, mapped into a table, mapping each URI to an identifier indicating whether the resulting mindmap node association type should be represented of a hierarchical, node-subnode relation or of a flat cross-node link. (Of course, it must be possible to transform the full set of “node-subnode associations” into a DAG, in the output mindmap. This would require some analysis, in the transform tool – may be difficult to do only with XSLT, even with XSL functions, so may serve to require a design of a stand-alone API and tool interface. Question: Is there a TMDM Gem for Ruby?)
